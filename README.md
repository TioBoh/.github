## 🦜 Welcome to Singapore Hokkien — Your New Superpower

Congratulations on starting your Singapore Hokkien journey! 🎉

If you already speak Mandarin (普通话, Pǔtōnghuà), you have a **huge head-start**. Singapore Hokkien (新加坡闽南话 / 福建话) shares thousands of root words with Mandarin and borrows heavily from it in the uniquely Singaporean way. But make no mistake — Hokkien has its own tones, sounds, grammar patterns, and a **vibrant identity** that is inseparable from the heartlands of Singapore.

By the end of this course you will be able to:

- Chat with your **ah gong** (grandfather) and **ah ma** (grandmother) in their mother tongue 👴👵
- Order **kopi-o** and **char kway teow** at the kopitiam like a true local ☕🍜
- Sing along to Hokkien pop songs and understand every word 🎵
- Navigate festivals — **Qing Ming**, **Ghost Month**, **Chinese New Year** — with the right greetings and vocabulary 🧧
- Understand the Hokkien layer hiding inside Singapore's own **Singlish** 🇸🇬

## 🇸🇬 Why Singapore Hokkien?

| Fact | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Speakers** | ~1.2 million Singaporeans identify Hokkien (Minnan) as their home dialect |
| **History** | Hokkien-speaking immigrants from Fujian province arrived in Singapore from the 1820s, making it the largest Chinese dialect group here |
| **Where you'll hear it** | Hawker centres, wet markets, kopitiams, temple festivals, family reunions, old Geylang and Chinatown shophouses |
| **Famous Singaporeans** | Lee Kuan Yew grew up speaking Hokkien before Mandarin became dominant; the Teochew and Hokkien merchant families built Boat Quay |
| **Cultural treasure** | With Singapore's Speak Mandarin Campaign (1979) replacing dialects in daily life, Singapore Hokkien is an endangered, precious heritage language |

> 💡 **Duolingo Tip:** Languages are never just grammar — they carry entire worlds. Learning Hokkien is learning *Singapore's soul*.

## 🎵 The Sounds of Singapore Hokkien

### Quick Comparison: Mandarin vs Singapore Hokkien Phonology

| Feature | Mandarin | Singapore Hokkien |
|---|---|---|
| **Tones** | 4 tones + neutral | 7 tones (8 in Classical) |
| **Finals** | No stop endings | -p, -t, -k, -h (glottal stop) endings exist (e.g., *bak* 肉, *chhit* 七) |
| **Nasalisation** | Limited | Vowels can be nasalised (ã, ẽ, ĩ) |
| **Initials** | b, p, m, f, d, t, n, l, g, k, h… | b (= Mandarin b), p (= Mandarin p), bh (voiced bilabial — not in Mandarin!), ts/ch, tsh/chh… |
| **Script** | Simplified/Traditional Chinese characters + Pinyin | Characters (shared with Mandarin) + POJ romanisation (白話字) |

### The 7 Tones at a Glance (POJ romanisation)

| Tone # | POJ mark | Example | Meaning | Mandarin feel |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | (no mark) | a | yes / ah | like 1st tone, high level |
| 2 | á | á | scold | rising, like Mandarin 2nd |
| 3 | à | à | (particle) | low falling |
| 4 | a̍ (stop) | pa̍k | tie | short, cut off with -p/-t/-k |
| 5 | â | ân | slow | rising-dipping |
| 7 | ā | bān | 10,000 | mid-level, like neutral tone |
| 8 | a̋ (high stop) | cha̋p | 10 | high, abrupt stop |

> 🎧 **Pro Tip for Mandarin speakers:** Tone 2 (rising) and Tone 5 (dip-rise) will feel familiar. The hardest to learn are the **stop tones** (4 and 8) because Mandarin has no final consonants of this type. Practise: *bak* (肉 meat), *chit* (一 one), *chhak* (刺 thorn).

## 📚 Unit 1: Greetings & Basics (问候语)

**Singapore Cultural Context:** Singaporeans of all ages still use Hokkien greetings informally — especially among older folks at the void deck, in lifts of HDB flats, and at the morning market (菜市 *chhài-chhī*).

### Core Vocabulary

| Hokkien (POJ) | Characters | Mandarin | English | Singapore Tip |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Lí hó | 你好 | Nǐ hǎo | Hello / How are you? | Used casually; older folk prefer this to Mandarin |
| Góa hó | 我好 | Wǒ hǎo | I'm fine / I'm well | |
| Lí chia̍h pá bōe? | 你食飽未？ | Nǐ chī bǎo le ma? | Have you eaten? | THE quintessential Singaporean greeting — asking about food IS caring |
| Chia̍h pá ah | 食飽啊 | Chī bǎo le | I've eaten (I'm full) | Standard reply; shows you're okay |
| Góa sī Sing-ka-pho lâng | 我是新加坡人 | Wǒ shì Xīnjiāpō rén | I am Singaporean | Sing-ka-pho = Singapore in Hokkien! |
| Lí tó-ūi lâng? | 你佗位人？ | Nǐ shì nǎ lǐ rén? | Where are you from? | |
| Siánn-mih? | 啥物？ | Shénme? | What? | Very common; you'll hear this everywhere |
| Ū-iánn | 有影 | Zhēn de ma? | Really? Is that so? | Uniquely Hokkien flavour, not in Mandarin |

### Mini Dialogue — At the Void Deck

> **Ah Huat:** Ah Soh! Lí hó bô?
> *(Ah Soh! How are you?)*
>
> **Ah Soh:** Hó hó lah! Lí chia̍h pá bōe?
> *(Good good lah! Have you eaten?)*
>
> **Ah Huat:** Chia̍h pá ah. Tng-á-jit ū beh khì bîn-nā chhài-chhī bô?
> *(I've eaten. Are you going to the morning market later?)*
>
> **Ah Soh:** Ū-iánn! Beh khì bé bak-bone thng.
> *(Really! Going to buy pork rib soup.)*

> 📌 **Cultural Note:** Notice the **-lah** particle. Hokkien and Singlish share sentence-final particles (*lah, leh, lor, mah, boh*). Most of Singlish's particles come directly from Hokkien and Cantonese — this is your first glimpse at Hokkien's influence on Singapore's national creole!

## 📚 Unit 2: At the Hawker Centre (熟食中心)

**Singapore Cultural Context:** The hawker centre (*熟食中心*, Hokkien: *jia̍t-si̍t tiong-sim*) is Singapore's communal dining room, UNESCO-listed in 2020. Knowing Hokkien here earns you real respect — and sometimes extra portions 😄.

### Essential Food Vocabulary

| Hokkien | Characters | English | Where to order |
|---|---|---|---|
| bah-chor mee | 肉脞面 | Minced pork noodles | Any old-school hawker centre |
| char kway teow | 炒粿條 | Fried flat rice noodles | Must try at Old Airport Road Food Centre |
| bak kut teh | 肉骨茶 | Pork rib herbal soup | Geylang, Joo Chiat |
| or luak | 蚵仔煎 | Oyster omelette | Clarke Quay, Geylang Serai night market |
| kopi-o | 咖啡烏 | Black coffee (no milk) | Any kopitiam; *o* means black in Hokkien |
| teh tarik | 拉茶 | Pulled milk tea | Malay-Hokkien fusion; every mamak stall |
| loti prata | 印度煎餅 | Roti prata | Hokkien-ified Malay/Tamil word! |
| bak | 肉 | Meat / Pork | *bak* = 肉 in Mandarin but ONLY means pork here |
| ikan (hioh) | 魚 | Fish | *hioh* is older Hokkien; *ikan* from Malay |
| tiam | 店 | Shop / Stall | As in *kopi-tiam* = coffee shop |

### Ordering Food: Key Phrases

| Hokkien (POJ) | Mandarin Bridge | English |
|---|---|---|
| Góa beh chia̍h… | Wǒ yào chī… | I want to eat… |
| Chit óa / Nn̄g óa | 一碗 / 兩碗 | One bowl / Two bowls |
| Bōe tio̍h | 買著 / 要買 | I'll take it / I'd like to buy |
| Jōa chē chîⁿ? | 多少錢？ | How much? |
| Phah pòe! | 打包！ | Pack it up to go! (Singlish: *dabao*) |
| Bē-sái | 無法 | Cannot / Not possible |
| Ē-sái | 可以 | Can / Possible |
| Siōng hó chia̍h! | 上好食！ | Delicious! / Best food! |

> 📌 **Cultural Note — *Kopitiam* Hokkien:** The entire kopitiam ordering system is Hokkien:
> - **kopi** = coffee (from Malay, rooted in Hokkien trading culture)
> - **o** (烏) = black (no milk)
> - **peh** / **gah dai** = with milk
> - **kosong** (from Malay) = no sugar
> - **siu dai** (少甜) = less sweet
> Ordering *"kopi-o siu dai"* is pure Hokkien-Malay fusion — Singapore in a cup ☕

## 📚 Unit 3: Family & Relationships (家庭)

**Singapore Cultural Context:** Multi-generational HDB families, Hungry Ghost Festival visits to grandparents' homes, and Chinese New Year reunion dinners all revolve around Hokkien kinship terms. These are *different* from Mandarin — and getting them right means everything to your elders.

### Kinship Terms (Hokkien vs Mandarin)

| Relationship | Hokkien | Mandarin | Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| Paternal grandfather | Ah-kong (阿公) | Yéye (爷爷) | *Ah-kong* is the Singlish version you hear everywhere |
| Paternal grandmother | Ah-má (阿嬤) | Nǎinai (奶奶) | Not to be confused with Mandarin *māma*! |
| Maternal grandfather | Góa-kong (外公) | Wàigōng | Similar to Mandarin |
| Maternal grandmother | Góa-má (外嬤) | Wàipó | |
| Father | Ah-pa (阿爸) | Bàba | *Ah-pa* = papa; Singlish uses this too |
| Mother | Ah-bú (阿母) | Māma | Note: *bú* not *má* for paternal side! |
| Elder brother | Ahiáⁿ (阿兄) | Gēge | |
| Elder sister | Achi (阿姊) | Jiějie | |
| Younger sibling | Sió-tī / Sió-mōe | Dìdi / Mèimei | |
| Husband | Ang (翁) | Lǎogōng | *Ang* is also in *ang-mo* (红毛 = Westerner)! |
| Wife | Bó (某) | Lǎopó | |

### Chinese New Year Phrases 🧧

| Hokkien | Mandarin | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Sin-nî hó! | 新年好！ | Happy New Year! |
| Kiong hee huat chye! | 恭喜發財！ | Wishing you prosperity! |
| Bān-sū jū-ì! | 萬事如意！ | May everything go as you wish! |
| Lí tiō chia̍h góa ê hong-pau! | 你要吃我的紅包！ | You must take my red packet! |
| Lóng ū huat ah! | 攏有發啊！ | We're all getting rich! |

> 📌 **Cultural Note:** During Chinese New Year, Singaporean Hokkien families exchange oranges (*kiat-á*, 桔仔) — a Hokkien tradition where *kiat* sounds like fortune. Mandarin uses *júzi*, but the New Year orange ritual in Singapore is distinctly Hokkien-rooted.

## 📚 Unit 4: Festivals & Temple Life (节庆与庙宇)

**Singapore Cultural Context:** Singapore's Hokkien community has a rich temple culture. Thian Hock Keng (天福宮) on Telok Ayer Street, built in 1839, is the oldest Hokkien temple in Singapore. The **Hungry Ghost Festival** (中元节 *Tiong-goân Cheh*) is celebrated with a uniquely Singaporean twist — *getai* street concerts, joss paper burning, and Hokkien opera (*koa-á-hì*).

### Festival Vocabulary

| Hokkien | Characters | English | Singapore Context |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tiong-goân Cheh | 中元節 | Hungry Ghost Festival | Seventh lunar month; big in Singapore |
| Choh-keng | 做功德 | Perform merit / rituals | Done at community centres and open fields |
| Sio kim-á | 燒金仔 | Burn joss paper | For ancestors; common in HDB void decks |
| Koa-á-hì | 歌仔戲 | Hokkien opera | Performed at empty seats for spirits |
| Getai | 歌台 | Live pop concert for spirits | Uniquely Singaporean; also for community! |
| Chhing-bîng | 清明 | Qing Ming / Tomb Sweeping Day | Families visit Mandai or Bukit Brown cemetery |
| Tiān-kong | 天公 | Heavenly Emperor (Jade Emperor) | Worshipped on 9th day of CNY — huge Hokkien tradition |
| Hiuⁿ | 香 | Incense | *Hiuⁿ* vs Mandarin *xiāng* — recognisably related! |

> 📌 **Cultural Note — Tiān-kong Worship (拜天公):** On the eve of the 9th day of Chinese New Year, Hokkien families stay up until midnight to pray to the Jade Emperor (Tiān-kong). This tradition is so important in Singapore that it rivals New Year's Day itself. Sugar cane stalks (*kam-chhe*, 甘蔗) are placed at the altar — a symbol of hiding from enemies, rooted in Hokkien historical legend. You'll see this in Chinatown and in Hokkien households across Singapore.

## 📚 Unit 5: Singlish — The Hokkien Connection 🇸🇬

**Singapore Cultural Context:** Singlish (Singapore Colloquial English) is not broken English — it is a **creole** language with Hokkien as one of its most important substrates. Understanding this unit will make Singlish click in a whole new way.

### Hokkien Words Now Part of Singlish

| Singlish Word | Hokkien Origin | Meaning | Example in Singlish |
|---|---|---|---|
| **lah** | 啦 (lah) | Sentence particle (softens, emphasises) | *"Don't be like that lah!"* |
| **lor** | 囉 (lo) | Resigned acceptance | *"Like that lor, what to do."* |
| **leh** | 咧 (leh) | Mild assertion / seeking agreement | *"This one very nice leh."* |
| **mah** | 嘛 (mah) | Obviousness particle | *"Of course lah, like that mah."* |
| **boh** | 無 (bô) | Question tag ("right?") | *"You going or boh?"* |
| **atas** | from Malay, reinforced by Hokkien | High-class, snobbish | *"That place very atas one."* |
| **kiasu** | 驚輸 (kiaⁿ-su) | Fear of losing out | *"Singapore people very kiasu."* |
| **kiasee** | 驚死 (kiaⁿ-sí) | Fear of death / overly cautious | *"Don't be so kiasee lah!"* |
| **ang mo** | 紅毛 (âng-mo) | Caucasian person | *"That ang mo can speak Hokkien!"* |
| **bo jio** | 無招 (bô-chiò) | Didn't invite me! | *"You all went without me? Bo jio!"* |
| **sian** | 散 (siān) | Bored / tired / fed up | *"So sian, nothing to do."* |
| **steady** (as in "steady bom pi pi") | — | Impressive / reliable | Half Hokkien spirit, pure Singapore 😄 |

> 💡 **Mandarin Bridge:** *Kiasu* (驚輸) = 怕輸 in Mandarin. *Kiasee* (驚死) = 怕死. The Hokkien word *kiaⁿ* means 怕 (to fear) — once you know this, dozens of Singlish words suddenly make sense!

## 📚 Unit 6: Getting Around Singapore (交通)

**Singapore Cultural Context:** The MRT, public buses, heartland shops, and taxi uncles — Hokkien is very much alive in how Singaporeans navigate their city.

### Places & Navigation

| Hokkien | Characters | English | Singapore Context |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cheng-Kong | 城中 | City centre / CBD | Downtown Singapore |
| Tng-a-chhut | 轉角出 | Around the corner / take the exit | Giving directions |
| Bûn-tâi | 巴士 | Bus | Borrowed: *bá-sī* in Hokkien = bus |
| Tih-tih khì | 直直去 | Go straight | Classic aunty direction 😄 |
| Chiàⁿ-pêng | 正爿 | Right side | *Pêng* = side; *chiàⁿ* = right |
| Tò-pêng | 倒爿 | Left side | *Tò* = left |
| Hia / Chia | 遐 / 遮 | There / Here | Fundamental spatial words |
| Tn̂g-á-keng | 唐人街 | Chinatown | Literally "Tang people's place" |

### Taxi Uncle Dialogue 🚕

> **You:** Chóa khì Tn̂g-á-keng, ē-sái bô?
> *(Can go to Chinatown?)*
>
> **Taxi Uncle:** Ē-sái! Lí sī Hokkien lâng ah?
> *(Can! You are Hokkien ah?)*
>
> **You:** Góa teh o̍h Hokkien — góa sī Mandarin lâng lah!
> *(I'm learning Hokkien — I'm a Mandarin speaker lah!)*
>
> **Taxi Uncle:** Hó lah! Bōe bái, bōe bái! Chóa khì!
> *(Good lah! Not bad, not bad! Let's go!)*

## 💬 A Final Word

Learning Singapore Hokkien is an act of cultural love. Every word you learn reconnects a thread between Singapore's present and its past — the Fujian immigrants who built Boat Quay, the *samsui* women who laid Singapore's foundations, the grandparents who whispered prayers at Thian Hock Keng.

Mandarin and Hokkien are cousins — sometimes the words look and sound so similar you'll have an *"aha!"* moment every lesson. Other times Hokkien will surprise you with sounds and structures that feel entirely new. Embrace both.

As Singaporeans say: **"Jia̍h pá bōe? Bōe lah — tio̍h o̍h!"**
*(Have you eaten yet? Not yet — still must learn!)*
